Section 03 of 05
The risk layer nobody budgets for.
The previous section covered the predictable cost. These are the episodic costs — events that aren't in the annual budget until they are. At a 17-person firm, a single incident in any of these categories can materially affect operations.
Workers' Compensation Claims
Workplace Injury Event
$20K – $80K
Average workers' comp claim in Florida runs $41,000 per incident (NCCI). Even in office environments — slip/fall, ergonomic injury, stress-related claims — one event eliminates a year of cost savings from a "cheap" hire. Premium rates also increase post-claim.
Employment Practices Liability
Wrongful Termination / Discrimination Claim
$40K – $200K+
The average EEOC charge settlement runs $40,000-$75,000 for a single-claimant case. Litigation defense alone can exceed $100,000 before a judgment. Florida is an at-will state, but documentation quality determines survivability at the EEOC level.
Leave Administration Exposure
FMLA / ADA Mishandling
$5K – $50K
Small employers under 50 employees are not covered by FMLA, but Florida's own leave laws and ADA overlap create traps. Improperly denying a qualifying leave or failing to engage in the interactive process can produce liability that dwarfs the cost of proper administration.
Wage and Hour Violations
Misclassification / Overtime Exposure
Back pay + 2x damages
FLSA misclassification exposes Rivani to two years of back overtime pay, doubled as liquidated damages, plus attorney's fees. The current Florida minimum wage is $14.00/hour (2025). A single non-exempt employee improperly classified costs more to correct than to fix upfront.
The math that matters for a 17-person firm: At a 5% annual probability of any single employment claim, a company of 17 employees has an 85% cumulative probability of facing one within 10 years — and a 58% probability within 5 years. These are not low-probability events. They are deferred events. The HR function is the risk mitigation instrument that reduces their severity and frequency.

An Asset Manager departs mid-portfolio.
An asset manager at a luxury CRE firm typically manages 3-6 active properties. They carry tenant relationships, deal structures, vendor contacts, and institutional knowledge of each asset. When they leave, none of that transfers cleanly.

Why CRE Is Different
In commercial real estate, an asset manager isn't just doing a job — they're holding relationships. Tenants, brokers, lenders, and vendors transact with people, not companies. When a key AM leaves, those relationships don't automatically transfer. A replacement needs 6-12 months to develop deal-level credibility from scratch. That's not a productivity ramp. That's a relationship rebuild — and it's a cost that doesn't appear in any turnover calculator.
A Project Manager exits during construction.
Construction PMs carry the thread on multiple active projects — vendor contracts, permit timelines, subcontractor relationships, and the day-to-day knowledge of what's actually happening on site. A mid-project exit doesn't just slow things down. It can stop them.

The Construction Timeline Problem
A 6-week delay on a $2.5M construction project is not just a scheduling issue. Delay costs in commercial construction typically run 0.5%-1.5% of project value per month. That's $12,500 to $37,500 per month of slippage — before you account for contractor penalty clauses, permit re-applications, or the carrying cost on financed projects. The PM departure isn't the cost. The timeline disruption is.

Your Capstone Thesis Line
"Why do small firms like Rivani sometimes resist hiring HR professionals?"
HR appears to be overhead until the moment it is not. The visible cost is the retainer. The invisible cost is the gap between what a firm thinks it spends on people and what it actually spends — a gap that widens with every unmanaged hire, every undocumented exit, and every deal that slips because the wrong person walked out the door.
